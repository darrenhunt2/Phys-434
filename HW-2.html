Which is more probable when rolling 2 six-sided dice: rolling snake eyes (two ones) or rolling sevens (dice sum to seven)? What is the ratio of the probabilities?

The first scenario is asking for the probability of dice #1 rolling a one AND dice #2 rolling a one. P(A and B) = P(A)P(B)

Each die has 6 sides, so the probability of rolling a one on either die alone is 1/6. There is only one possible combination to achieve this outcome.

In [2]:
# P of rolling 2 ones
P_2_ones = (1/6)*(1/6)
print("Probability of rolling 2 ones : " + str("{0:.5f}".format(P_2_ones)))

Probability of rolling 2 ones : 0.02778

The probabilty of rolling a sum of seven has different combinations to achieve the same sum, so there are more possible chances for this outcome to occur. The (unique) combinations of the two dice are:

1 and 6; 2 and 5; 3 and 4.

And since each of these combinations can occur twice,

6 and 1; 5 and 2; 4 and 3,

there are a total of 6 possible combinations leading to the same outcome sum of 7. Therefore, the calculation for this probability takes the probabilities of getting N as a value on a 6 sided die (always 1/6 for equal chances to roll), times that again (for the second die) times 6 (for the 6 different combinations possible).

The mean of the probability distribution is the mean of the expected values, not the mean of the probabilities. Our expected values are the possible sum outcomes of rolling two dice (stored in the array sums above). To find the mean, I multiplied the possible sum times its corresponding probability, then summed up these results. The variance followed a statistical formula for variance of a probability distribution and describes generally how far out the distribution spreads from the mean--if the expected value is about 7, our most probable outcome, it makes sense the distribution will spread about 5 in either direction.

# a function to convolve the rolled dice probabilities N given times

def average_dice(p_dice, N):
    # parameter dice: array of probabilities for each side of the die
    # parameter N: integer number of times to convolve
    avg = p_dice
    for i in range(N):
        avg = np.convolve(p_dice,avg)
    vals = np.linspace(1,6,len(avg))
    
    # for corresponding Gaussian
    mean = sum(vals * avg)
    std = np.sqrt(sum((vals ** 2) * avg) - (mean ** 2))
    return vals, avg, mean, std

The possible average values include 1 through 6, both integer and float, since an average calculation includes continuous possibilities. The average of 10 rolled dice approaches the general Gaussian shape, and will continue to approach a Gaussian distribution the more times we average. This corresponds to the Central Limit Theorem, which dictates that the probability distribution will converge to a Gaussian shape when averaged or summed many times. Since our initial distribution already had Gaussian-like behavior, it was able to converge quickly--even at 10 dice, we can see the skeleton of a normal distribution. For a large N convolutions, such as the second graph for 200 dice, we see the average fills the Gaussian shape. Additionally, the width of the distribution has changed. The most probable outcome for large N remains at the peak, but the other probabilities to the left and right of the mean fall off much quicker given enough trials.

Show that the sum and average of an initially Gaussian distribution is also a Gaussian (can be analytic or numerical). How does the standard deviation of the resulting sum or average Guassian change? This is a hugely important result. Explore what this means for integrating a signal over time.

To do this, we need to start with a Gaussian distribution of data. Additionally, a semilog plot will help determine the true Gaussian nature, as it should appear to be a parabola for semilog axes.

As we sum together distributions which are already Gaussian, the standard deviation and means do not change; only the height of their peaks increases. The averaging by convolution of distributions that are already Gaussian maintains the normal shape, while decreasing the standard deviation and variance from the mean. So as we perform convolution an increasing N times, the distribution becomes a Gaussian with less and less spread. In terms of signals and observations, this demonstrates that if we average our background enough times, we are able to increase our confidence in what is and what is not signal since the spread decreases. Additionally, if we know that our data follows a Gaussian distribution from the start, we can be confident that it will quickly converge to a probability distribution with a very high level of certainty.
